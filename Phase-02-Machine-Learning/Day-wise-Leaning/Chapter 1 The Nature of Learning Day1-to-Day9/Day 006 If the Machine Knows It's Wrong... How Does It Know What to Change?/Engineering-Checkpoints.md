
# Engineering Checkpoint

## 1. What new mental model did I build today?

Today, I understood that improvement is not just about knowing that a mistake happened. It's about identifying which part needs adjustment and making controlled changes instead of changing everything.

---

## 2. What misconception did I correct today?

Earlier, I thought bigger mistakes always required bigger changes everywhere. Today, I realized that even a large problem may be solved by adjusting only the specific part responsible for it, while leaving everything else unchanged.

---

## 3. What question is still confusing me?

I understand that the machine needs to identify what should change, but I'm curious about how it decides which part is responsible for the mistake when many parts work together.

---

## 4. If I had to teach today's lesson in 2 minutes, what would I say?

Imagine you're riding a bicycle.

You notice that you're slowly drifting toward the left side of the road.

Would you completely change how you ride?

Would you stop pedaling, move the seat, change the tires, and replace the handlebars?

Of course not.

You make a small adjustment to the handlebars because that's the part causing the problem.

After a moment, you check again.

If you're still drifting a little, you make another small adjustment.

Over time, these small corrections keep you moving in the right direction.

Learning works the same way.

When we make a mistake, the goal isn't to change everything. It's to identify what needs adjustment, make a controlled change, observe the result, and continue improving step by step.

That's how steady improvement happens—in cycling, cooking, coding, and eventually in Machine Learning as well.
