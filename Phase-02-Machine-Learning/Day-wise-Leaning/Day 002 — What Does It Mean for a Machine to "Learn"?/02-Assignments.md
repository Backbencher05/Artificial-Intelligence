# Part A — Memorization vs Learning

## 1. Cooking

### What would memorization look like?

Remembering the exact cooking time for every dish you've ever made.

### What would true learning look like?

Understanding how heat, ingredients, and cooking time affect the food, allowing you to cook even unfamiliar recipes.

### Why is learning more useful?

You can adapt to different ingredients, cookware, or quantities instead of depending on fixed instructions.

---

## 2. Cricket

### What would memorization look like?

Remembering how every previous ball was bowled.

### What would true learning look like?

Recognizing patterns in a bowler's action, field placement, and game situation to anticipate the next delivery.

### Why is learning more useful?

Every match is different. Learning helps you respond to new situations rather than replay old ones.

---

## 3. Software Engineering

### What would memorization look like?

Copying code snippets or remembering solutions to specific bugs.

### What would true learning look like?

Understanding programming principles, system design, and debugging techniques so you can solve new problems you've never encountered before.

### Why is learning more useful?

Technology changes constantly. Principles remain valuable even when frameworks and tools evolve.

---

## 4. Gym Training

### What would memorization look like?

Following the same workout routine every week without understanding why.

### What would true learning look like?

Understanding progressive overload, recovery, nutrition, and how your body responds to training.

### Why is learning more useful?

You can adjust your training based on your goals, fitness level, and recovery instead of blindly following a routine.

## 5. Driving

### What would memorization look like?

Remembering every road and traffic signal you've driven through.

### What would true learning look like?

Understanding traffic patterns, safe driving principles, and how to react to unexpected situations.

### Why is learning more useful?

You can drive safely even on roads you've never visited before.

# Part B — Think Like an Engineer

## Q1. Why is memorization insufficient for solving real-world problems?

Real-world situations are constantly changing. It's impossible to memorize every possible scenario that might occur. Memorization only works when a new situation exactly matches something seen before. Learning, on the other hand, identifies underlying patterns that can be applied to unfamiliar situations, making it far more useful in dynamic environments.

---

## Q2. Why do we expect an ML model to make predictions for inputs it has never seen before?

The purpose of an ML model is not to repeat answers for known examples but to make informed predictions for new data. If it could only respond to examples it had already seen, it would simply be a lookup table rather than a learning system. The value of Machine Learning lies in applying learned patterns to unseen situations.

---

## Q3. What do you think "generalization" might mean?

Based on today's lesson, I think **generalization** is the ability to apply patterns learned from past examples to new examples that were never part of the training process. Instead of remembering individual cases, the model understands the underlying relationships and uses them to make accurate predictions in unfamiliar situations.

---

## Q4. If an ML model performs perfectly on training data but poorly on new data, has it truly learned? Why or why not?

No. Perfect performance on training data alone suggests the model may have memorized the examples rather than learned the underlying pattern. True learning is demonstrated when the model performs well on new, unseen data because that shows it has captured the general relationships instead of just recalling past observations.
