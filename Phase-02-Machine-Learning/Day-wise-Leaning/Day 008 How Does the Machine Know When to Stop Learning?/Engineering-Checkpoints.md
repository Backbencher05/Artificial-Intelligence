
# Engineering Checkpoint

## 1. What new mental model did I build today?

Today, I understood that good engineering is not about improving forever. It's about recognizing when a system has reached a point where further effort brings very little additional value. Knowing when to stop is part of building an efficient solution.

---

## 2. What misconception did I correct today?

Earlier, I believed that continuous improvement always leads to better results. Today, I realized that after a certain point, more changes can waste time, consume resources, or even reduce the quality of the final outcome.

---

## 3. What question is still confusing me?

I understand that improvement should eventually stop, but I'm curious about how we can confidently decide that a system has improved enough and doesn't need further changes.

---

## 4. If I had to teach today's lesson in 2 minutes, what would I say?

Imagine you're cleaning your room before guests arrive.

At first, picking up clothes, dusting the furniture, and organizing your desk make a huge difference.

Then you notice a tiny speck of dust on a shelf. You clean it.

After that, you spend another hour searching for even smaller imperfections that nobody will notice.

At some point, you're putting in a lot more effort without making the room meaningfully better.

Many real-life activities work the same way. Whether you're studying, exercising, cooking, or building software, the biggest improvements usually happen early. As you get closer to your goal, each additional improvement becomes smaller and requires more effort.

Good engineering isn't about improving forever. It's about knowing when the result is good enough to achieve its purpose and when it's time to stop and move on.
