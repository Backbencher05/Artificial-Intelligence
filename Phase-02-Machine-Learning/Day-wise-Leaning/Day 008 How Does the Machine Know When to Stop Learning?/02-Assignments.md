# Part A — Knowing When to Stop

## 1. Studying

### What is the goal?

To prepare well for an exam.

### What improvements happen at first?

Each study session improves understanding and confidence.

### When do improvements begin to slow down?

After the important concepts are well understood and only minor details remain.

### Why is continuing beyond that point not useful (or harmful)?

Studying for too long can lead to fatigue, reduced concentration, and less effective learning.

### How is this similar to Machine Learning?

Improvement is valuable in the beginning, but after a certain point, additional effort may provide very little benefit.

## 2. Exercise

### What is the goal?

To become stronger and healthier.

### What improvements happen at first?

Strength, endurance, and fitness improve steadily.

### When do improvements begin to slow down?

After consistent training, progress becomes more gradual.

### Why is continuing beyond that point not useful (or harmful)?

Excessive training without enough recovery can lead to exhaustion or injury.

Continuous improvement is important, but pushing too far without balance can reduce overall performance.

## 5. Software Optimization

### What is the goal?

To improve the performance of an application.

### What improvements happen at first?

# Part B — Think Like an Engineer

## Q1. Why isn't "keep improving forever" always a good strategy?

Improvement requires time, effort, and resources. After a certain point, the benefits become very small while the cost continues to grow. Continuing indefinitely may waste resources or even make the system worse.

---

## Q2. Why might tiny improvements eventually become less valuable?

The biggest improvements usually happen early. As the system gets better, each additional improvement often has a smaller impact than the previous one. Eventually, the effort required becomes greater than the value gained.

---

## Q3. Based on today's lesson, how would you decide whether it's time to stop improving a system?

I would compare the benefit of further improvements with the time, effort, and resources they require. If additional changes produce only tiny gains while consuming significant effort or introducing unnecessary risks, it's a good indication that the system is ready.

---

## Q4. Do you think "perfect" is always the goal in engineering? Why or why not?

No. Engineering is about building solutions that are effective, reliable, and practical. Chasing perfection can consume excessive time and resources without providing meaningful additional value. A well-balanced solution that meets its purpose is often better than an endlessly optimized one.
