# Part A — Everyday Feedback Loops

## 1. Learning to Swim

### What is the goal?

To swim confidently and correctly.

### What action is performed?

Practicing different swimming strokes.

### What feedback is received?

The instructor points out mistakes like incorrect breathing or arm movements, and the swimmer notices whether they stay balanced in the water.

### How does that feedback help improve the next attempt?

The swimmer corrects those mistakes and performs the stroke more effectively the next time.

### What would happen if there were no feedback?

The swimmer would likely repeat the same mistakes and improve much more slowly.

---

## 2. Playing Chess

### What is the goal?

To make better moves and win the game.

### What action is performed?

Choosing and playing a move.

### What feedback is received?

The opponent's response and the resulting board position reveal whether the move was beneficial or harmful.

### How does that feedback help improve the next attempt?

The player begins to recognize better strategies and avoids repeating poor decisions.

### What would happen if there were no feedback?

The player would have no way of knowing which moves were effective and which were mistakes.

---

## 3. Cooking

### What is the goal?

To prepare a tasty meal.

### What action is performed?

Cooking a dish using a chosen recipe.

### What feedback is received?

The taste, texture, aroma, and comments from others.

### How does that feedback help improve the next attempt?

The cook adjusts the ingredients, seasoning, or cooking time to produce a better result.

### What would happen if there were no feedback?

The same cooking mistakes would continue, and the dish would rarely improve.

## 4. Writing Code

### What is the goal?

To develop software that works correctly.

### What action is performed?

Writing and running the code.

### What feedback is received?

Compiler errors, test results, application behavior, and code reviews.

### How does that feedback help improve the next attempt?

The developer fixes bugs, improves logic, and writes more reliable code.

### What would happen if there were no feedback?

The developer wouldn't know whether the program works correctly or where improvements are needed.

# Part B — Think Like an Engineer

## Q1. Why is simply knowing "right" or "wrong" often insufficient for learning?

Knowing only whether something is right or wrong doesn't explain what needs to change. To improve, we need feedback that helps us understand where we went wrong and guides us toward a better approach on the next attempt.

---

## Q2. Why is measuring how wrong a prediction is more useful than just knowing it is wrong?

Not all mistakes are equally serious. Understanding how far we are from the correct answer helps us decide how much we need to improve. Small mistakes may require small adjustments, while larger mistakes may require bigger changes.

---

## Q3. Based on today's lesson, why do you think ML training is an iterative process?

Learning doesn't happen in a single attempt. It follows a cycle: make an attempt, receive feedback, make improvements, and try again. Repeating this cycle allows performance to improve gradually over time.

---

## Q4. Imagine a machine that never receives feedback after making predictions. Can it improve? Why or why not?

No. Without feedback, the machine has no way of knowing whether its predictions are good or bad. If it cannot evaluate its previous attempts, it has no basis for making improvements in future attempts.

## 4. If I had to teach today's lesson in 2 minutes, what would I say?

Imagine you're learning to play basketball.

You take a shot.

If nobody tells you whether it went in, whether it was too short, too long, or too far to the left, you'll probably keep shooting the same way every time.

Now imagine someone gives you feedback after every shot.

"This one was too strong."

"This one was a little to the left."

"This one was almost perfect."

With every attempt, you make a small improvement based on that feedback.

Over time, your shots become more accurate.

Learning works like this in many areas of life. We try something, observe the result, make adjustments, and try again. The cycle of **attempt → feedback → improvement** is what helps us get better.

The same idea will guide everything we study in Machine Learning. Before a machine can improve, it must first receive feedback about how well it performed.
